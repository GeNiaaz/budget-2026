FY2025 ended with a better-than-expected $15.1 billion surplus (1.9% of GDP) due to strong corporate tax collections and robust property and vehicle demand. FY2026 projects a more modest $8.5 billion surplus (1% of GDP). The Net Investment Returns Contribution ‚Äî essentially returns from reserves managed by GIC and Temasek ‚Äî contributes $28.5 billion, a critical pillar that more than offsets the primary deficit. PM Wong described this as a balanced approach, keeping "dry powder" in reserve for potential global shocks during this first year of the new electoral term.

üéØ Four National AI Missions

Coordinated AI transformation in advanced manufacturing, connectivity & logistics, finance, and healthcare. These are intended to be comprehensive sectoral pushes with regulatory sandboxes, R&D support, and clear deployment pathways ‚Äî not just pilot projects. A "Champions of AI" programme will support companies undertaking end-to-end AI integration.

üìö For Workers

SkillsFuture website redesigned for clearer AI pathways. Selected AI course takers get 6 months of premium AI tool access (e.g. paid-tier subscriptions). TechSkills Accelerator expanding beyond tech into accountancy and legal. AI literacy strengthened across all IHLs.

üèóÔ∏è For Businesses

AI expenditure now qualifies under the Enterprise Innovation Scheme at 400% tax deduction (cap $50K). Expanded Productivity Solutions Grant for AI tools. Why it matters: The government is positioning AI not as a future consideration but as a present-day national priority. Whether you're a developer, manager, or non-tech professional, AI literacy is becoming a baseline expectation. The free premium tools offer is a tangible incentive to start now.

Career, Jobs & Workforce

Structural changes to workforce support, higher foreign worker salary thresholds, and stronger investment in lifelong learning.

üîÑ SkillsFuture + Workforce SG ‚Üí New Merged Agency

SkillsFuture Singapore (under MOE) and Workforce Singapore (under MOM) will merge into a single statutory board jointly overseen by both ministries. The new agency will be a one-stop shop for skills training, career guidance, and job matching. Previously, these were fragmented across two separate organisations. For workers, this means more seamless support for career planning, upskilling, and job transitions ‚Äî especially important in an era of accelerating technological change.

Higher qualifying salaries mean companies must pay more for foreign hires, which can support upward pressure on local wages. The Local Qualifying Salary increase (minimum salary for locals in firms hiring foreign workers) raises the floor for lower-wage workers across the board.

üìà Progressive Wage Credit Scheme

Government co-funding raised from 20% ‚Üí 30% and extended two more years to 2028. The minimum wage increase required to qualify also rises from $100 to $200, rewarding meaningful pay bumps over token adjustments.

üéì SkillsFuture Level-Up Programme

Over 600,000 individuals took up SkillsFuture courses in 2025. The Level-Up Programme for mid-career workers will be extended. Mid-Career Training Allowance now available for part-time learners too, with coverage expanding to more industry-relevant courses.

üë¥ For Senior Workers

The retirement age will be raised to 64 from 1 July 2026 (it was 63). CPF contribution rates for workers aged 55‚Äì65 will increase in 2027 (2.5% total for ages 55‚Äì60; 2% total for ages 60‚Äì65). The CPF Transition Offset will cover half of the increased employer contributions. The Senior Employment Credit is extended to end-2027. These changes affect both older workers and the employers who hire them.

05

CPF & Retirement

New investment options for long-term wealth building, plus targeted support for seniors with insufficient savings.

üìä New Life-Cycle Investment Scheme (Launching H1 2028)

CPF Board will introduce a voluntary investment scheme offering life-cycle products from 2‚Äì3 reputable providers. The core concept: your portfolio automatically starts with higher equity exposure (more growth potential) when you're younger, then progressively shifts toward lower-risk assets as you approach retirement ‚Äî a "glide path" approach used widely in retirement planning globally.


This complements the existing CPF Investment Scheme (CPFIS) and is designed for members who want potentially higher returns but prefer not to actively manage their investments. Choices will be kept simple and costs low. All investment products carry risk, and returns are subject to market conditions. Further details will be announced closer to launch.

üí∞ CPF Top-Up for Seniors

Singaporeans aged 50+ (born 1976 or earlier) with CPF retirement savings below the Basic Retirement Sum will receive up to $1,500 in December 2026. Tiered by savings balance and property value ‚Äî lower balances get larger top-ups.

‚¨ÜÔ∏è CPF Rate Increases (2027)

Workers aged 55‚Äì60: total increase of 2.5 percentage points. Workers aged 60‚Äì65: total increase of 2 percentage points. Government provides the CPF Transition Offset to cover half of the employer's increased contribution for the first year.

For younger workers: The life-cycle scheme is arguably the most significant long-term wealth move in this budget. With decades of compounding ahead, even small improvements in CPF returns add up substantially. If you've been letting your CPF sit in OA at 2.5%, this will offer a structured alternative with higher expected returns. Watch for details in late 2027.

üè¢ Corporate Tax Relief

A 40% corporate income tax rebate for YA2026 (down from 50% in YA2025), capped at $30,000 per company. Active companies that employed at least one local worker in 2025 receive a minimum benefit of $1,500 via a CIT rebate cash grant. This is the third consecutive year of rebates ‚Äî a signal that the government recognises ongoing cost pressures for businesses, especially SMEs.

üöÄ Startups & Growth Capital

$1 billion added to Startup SG Equity, now expanded to cover growth-stage companies (previously seed-stage only). A new workgroup will develop strategies to position Singapore as a leading hub for growth capital. Enterprise Financing Scheme caps of $10‚Äì30M per facility lifted from April 2026.

üìä SGX & Capital Markets

$1.5B added to the Equity Market Development Programme (total now $6.5B). Plans for a dual-listing bridge between SGX and Nasdaq. Listing rules to be streamlined for high-growth companies. A second $1.5B Anchor Fund for high-quality public listings. STI climbed 22.67% in 2025, its best year since 2009.

üåè International Expansion Support

SMEs going overseas get up to 70% of eligible costs covered (non-SMEs: 50%). The Market Readiness Assistance grant is enhanced to support not just entering new markets but also deepening activities in existing overseas markets. Engagement is being stepped up with fast-growing markets in Latin America, Africa, and the Middle East. Regionally, the Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone (JS-SEZ) and Batam-Bintan-Karimun developments are advancing.

Transport, Tax & Lifestyle Changes

Immediate-impact changes that affect your wallet.

üöó Cars ‚Äî PARF Rebate Cut Cost ‚Üë

The Preferential Additional Registration Fee (PARF) rebate has been reduced by 45 percentage points, and the cap halved from $60,000 to $30,000. This dramatically increases the depreciation cost of petrol and petrol-hybrid vehicles over a 10-year COE cycle. The move makes EVs relatively more attractive and accelerates Singapore's goal of 100% cleaner-energy vehicles by 2040.

üö¨ Tobacco +20% Effective Immediately

A 20% increase in tobacco excise duty took effect on 12 February 2026, the day of the budget announcement. This is an immediate cost increase for smokers. The government frames this as a public health measure.

üå± Carbon Tax ‚Äî $45/ton Up from $25

The carbon tax nearly doubled from $25/ton to $45/ton. This affects energy-intensive businesses and may trickle down to consumer electricity costs. The Energy Efficiency Grant has been extended to March 2027 to help companies invest in energy-efficient equipment.

üéÅ Charitable Giving Extended

The 250% tax deduction for qualifying donations to IPCs and eligible institutions is extended three years, now running to 31 December 2029. The Corporate Volunteer Scheme is also extended. A new $50M SG Partnerships Fund supports ground-up community initiatives.

Family Support

Broader and deeper financial support for families raising children, including enhanced subsidies and direct cash transfers.

üë∂ Child LifeSG Credits ‚Äî $500 per child

Every Singaporean child aged 12 and below in 2026 receives $500 in Child LifeSG credits, usable for daily household expenses like groceries and utilities. Disbursement: July 2026 for children aged 1‚Äì12; April 2027 for children born in 2026. This follows a similar $500 payout in Budget 2025.

The Global Context

Why the budget is shaped the way it is ‚Äî PM Wong's framing of the world Singapore now operates in.

PM Wong opened his budget speech by acknowledging that the US-led multilateral order that underpinned nearly 80 years of global stability is shifting. Trade flows are becoming selective, partnerships more strategic, and geopolitics increasingly tense. While Singapore achieved 5% GDP growth in 2025 (beating forecasts), PM Wong cautioned that such tailwinds may not continue, with growth expected to moderate to 2‚Äì4% in 2026.


His core message: Singapore cannot afford to be a passive spectator. The budget focuses on diversifying trade relationships beyond traditional partners ‚Äî deepening ties with Latin America, Africa, the Middle East, and regional partners through the JS-SEZ and Batam-Bintan-Karimun FTZ. Singapore is also signing an Agreement on Trade in Essential Supplies with New Zealand later in 2026. Defence spending remains at ~3% of GDP with openness to increase as needed.

The big picture: Budget 2026 is less about handouts and more about structural repositioning. The AI push, R&D investment, capital markets development, and workforce restructuring all point toward building resilience for a world where growth doesn't come as easily. The cost-of-living measures are the near-term cushion; the economic strategy is the long game.
